Planning a Meal

If you have spent any time cooking for a large group, or preparing anything more complex than soup or sandwiches, you know that there is a lot of preparation that must be done and a lot of details to track for the meal to be a success. Planning and preparing a complex meal really is a very nice example of Project Management.

For this activity you will choose a project management method and then plan a complex meal using it.

Activity Instructions

  1. Choose a Method

    Begin by selecting a project management method (Sample list â€“ You can add your own to this list if you do not see it here). Do some research and reading on the method, and then describe it in your own words.

  2. Plan a Meal

    Apply your understanding by describing how you would use your selected method to organize, prepare, deliver, and clean up a balanced meal for 3 or more people. Balanced means multiple food groups are represented, ie meat or other protein, vegetables, fruit, possibly grains or dairy, and an optional dessert. Review the following "charter" for more details.

    Dear friend,

    Welcome to the neighborhood dinner club. We are excited to have you join. There is just one last requirement that must be met: we need to assess your ability to plan and execute a dinner party. In order to show this you need to prepare and serve a meal to the managing board members. If you meal meets our expectations...you are in!

    Here are a few details you will need:

    1. You will host us at your place. Let us know what time we should arrive. We will expect the meal to be served promptly after our arrival. You should plan on at least three guests, but can invite more if you would like. You may server the meal indoors or outdoors.
    2. The menu should be for a balanced meal and should be prepared mostly from basic ingredients...if you serve us Ramen or Macaroni and Cheese it had better be home made and delicious! It should involve some sort of protein, and good helpings of fruits and vegetables. Dessert is a must! Drinks are optional, but non-alcholic only please!
    3. Flexibility is important when hosting a dinner party. Bonus points will be given if consideration has been given to adaptation. Sometimes you get late cancels, someone brings an unexpected guest, or has dietary restrictions they did not inform you of (did you ask?)...How will you accommodate these changes and make sure you have enough food?

    You will need to consider the following as the lead on this:

    • Consider each phase of the meal: planning, shopping, inviting, preparation (cooking and setup), eating, and cleanup. Make sure you allow enough time for each phase. Did you consider the preparation of each part of the meal you may be making to ensure they are all ready at the same time? Part of hosting is focussing your attention on your guests once they arrive. Will your plan allow you to do this?
    • The meal does not have to be gourmet or expensive. It should be prepared mostly from scratch however. Keep it simple. Make sure that it fits into your budget. Estimate the costs for the meal once you have decided on the menu.
    • Who will be helping you pull this off? What will be their roles?
    • How will you keep me and everyone else involved up to date on your progress and what they should be aware of? How will you extend your invitations and allow for RSVPs from each invitee?
    • How will you keep track of your progress so you know if you are on track to succeed?
    • What risks will you face? What could derail your plans and cause the dinner to fail? How will you mitigate those risks?
    • You should have a detailed plan to me no later than then second week in the semester containing the information above.

    I look forward to eating with you!

    Board Director

  3. Execute your plan

    Once you have your plan made...execute it! You should follow the plan to actually invite 3+ people to dinner, prepare the meal for them, and then clean it up.

  4. Post Mortem

    At the end of a project it is useful to talk about how things went...what went well, what could have been improved, did you stay on budget both with time and money? What was your stress level at each phase? What could be done to reduce the stress on you at each point? This is often called a 'Post Mortem'. Evaluate your project and write up your own Post mortem. You might even consider getting your guests input on this as well.
